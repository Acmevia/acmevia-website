"How much for an LMS?" is the first question every institute asks, and the honest answer is the one nobody likes: it depends. But *what* it depends on is completely knowable — and once you can see the cost drivers, you can read any quotation and tell whether you're being charged for substance or for a sales margin.

Here's how we think about pricing a [learning management system](/products/lms/), whether you build it with us or with anyone else.

## What actually moves the price

**1. How many students, and how they behave at once.** A system for 300 students is a different engineering problem from one for 30,000 — not because the screens change, but because concurrency does. Two hundred students opening the same exam at 9:00 a.m. on a slow mobile connection is the real test. Ask any vendor to demo that scenario, not a tidy ten-record sandbox.

**2. How many roles need their own view.** Students, teachers, parents, admins, finance — each role is a separate set of permissions, screens, and rules. A student-and-teacher system is cheap. Add parent portals, fee visibility, and an admin hierarchy and the surface area grows. Roles, not features, are usually the bigger cost line.

**3. Whether exams are real exams.** "Online quizzes" are easy. Timed papers with auto-save, graceful resume after a dropped connection, question banks, and automated grading that produces a real result sheet — that is where serious engineering time goes. It's also where the largest block of your staff's admin time disappears, so it's usually worth paying for.

**4. Migration and data.** Five years of student records, past results, and fee history rarely live in a clean spreadsheet. Getting them in — mapped, de-duplicated, verified — is real work that a low quote quietly skips, leaving you to do it by hand later.

## The costs nobody puts in the quote

The sticker price is the licence or the build. The *total* cost includes three things that are easy to forget:

- **Hosting and scale.** A cloud LMS costs money to run, and that cost rises with usage. A per-institute monthly fee usually folds this in; a one-time build may not.
- **Training and adoption.** An LMS nobody uses is an expensive login page. Budget for getting your staff genuinely comfortable with it — that is the difference between software you bought and software you use.
- **Support after month one.** Ask exactly what support costs once the launch glow fades. A real response commitment belongs in the contract, not in a friendly verbal promise.

## Build vs. white-label

You have two honest paths. A fully bespoke LMS, designed from zero around your institute, costs the most and takes the longest — right when you may not need anything unique yet. A [white-label product](/products/lms/) that's already been proven across dozens of institutes costs a fraction and goes live in weeks, carrying your name, logo, and domain. For most schools and tuition classes, the white-label route is the better first move; bespoke is what you graduate to when your process genuinely diverges from everyone else's.

## A fair way to compare quotes

Line the quotes up and ask each vendor the same five things: what happens at your real peak load, how exams behave when the internet drops mid-paper, who migrates your historical data, what training is included, and what support costs from month two. The cheapest quote almost always turns out to be the one that answered the fewest of these — the cost didn't vanish, it just moved onto your plate.
