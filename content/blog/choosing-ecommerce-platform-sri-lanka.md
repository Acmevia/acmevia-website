Choosing an e-commerce platform feels like choosing a brand — Shopify, WooCommerce, something custom — but that's the wrong frame. The platform is just the foundation. What decides whether your store makes money is how well it handles three unglamorous things: taking payment, telling the truth about stock, and not falling over as you grow. Here's how to choose an [e-commerce build](/services/#ecommerce) with those in mind, in the Sri Lankan context specifically.

## Payments come first, not last

In Sri Lanka this is where many stores quietly lose sales. Your platform has to support the local payment gateways your customers actually use, handle cards cleanly, and — increasingly — support cash on delivery without turning fulfilment into chaos. If you also sell to buyers abroad, you need international payment options alongside the local ones. Before you fall in love with a theme, confirm the platform integrates the exact gateways you need. A beautiful store that can't take a customer's preferred payment is just an expensive catalogue.

## Stock has to tell the truth

The fastest way to lose a customer's trust is to sell them something you don't have. If your store and your warehouse (or your [ERP](/services/#erp-systems)) keep separate stock counts, oversells are a matter of *when*, not *if*. The platform has to sync inventory with wherever your real stock lives, so the number a customer sees is the number you actually hold. For any business selling on more than one channel, this single capability matters more than the entire theme gallery.

## The checkout is where money leaks

Most abandoned carts aren't a pricing problem — they're a friction problem. Every extra field, every forced account creation, every confusing step sheds buyers who were ready to pay. When you evaluate a platform, walk through its checkout on a phone as if you were a rushed customer. Count the taps from cart to confirmation. Fewer decisions, fewer fields, fewer surprises about delivery cost — that's the difference between a store that converts and one that just gets browsed.

## Matching the platform to your stage

- **Hosted platforms (e.g. Shopify).** Fastest to launch, least to maintain, monthly fee, and you live within the platform's rules. Great when you want to start selling now and your needs are fairly standard.
- **WooCommerce / open-source.** More control and no per-sale platform tax, at the cost of managing hosting, updates, and plugins yourself. A strong middle path when you want ownership without a full custom build.
- **Custom-built.** The right call when your selling process is genuinely unusual — complex product configuration, tight ERP integration, a marketplace with multiple sellers — and a standard platform would mean fighting the tool. More cost up front, but the cheaper path when the store *is* the business.

## Decide from your real requirements

Write down, honestly, what your store must do: which payment methods, where stock lives, how many products, whether you sell on other channels, and how unusual your process really is. Take *that* list to each option, not a brand name.
