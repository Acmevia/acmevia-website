"We need an app" is one of the most expensive sentences a business can say without checking. Sometimes it's exactly right. Often, what's actually needed is a website that works beautifully on a phone — which costs less, ships faster, and skips the app stores entirely. Before you commit to a [mobile app](/services/#mobile-app-development), it's worth knowing which problem you're really solving.

## What a mobile website is best at

If your goal is that people on phones can find you, read about you, and get in touch, you want a fast, responsive website — not an app. Nobody downloads an app to check your services once. A mobile web experience is discoverable on Google, opens instantly from a link, needs no install, and updates the moment you publish. For marketing, catalogues, content, and lead capture, this is almost always the right answer, and building an app instead just adds a download step between you and the customer.

## Where a native app genuinely earns its place

An app is worth the extra cost and complexity when it does things a browser can't do well:

- **It works offline or on bad connections.** Field agents, delivery drivers, and warehouse staff can't depend on signal. An app that captures orders or proof-of-delivery offline and syncs later is doing something a website can't. This is the strongest single reason to go native.
- **It needs deep device features.** Continuous GPS, the camera as a scanner, Bluetooth hardware, reliable push notifications tied to real events — these are where apps pull ahead.
- **People use it every day.** An app icon on the home screen makes sense for a tool used daily. For something touched twice a year, that icon is just clutter nobody installed.
- **It's for your team, not the public.** Internal tools — a driver app, a sales-force app, a stocktake app — are often the clearest case, because you can require the install and the daily use is a given.

## The honest middle ground

There's a third option people forget: a well-built web app that behaves like an app in a browser — installable, fast, works on any device — without two separate app-store builds to maintain. It won't match a native app for offline depth or hardware access, but for many "we need an app" requests it delivers most of the value at a fraction of the cost and time. The right question is which capabilities you actually need, not which sounds more impressive.

## Decide from the use case, not the trend

Run your real scenario through three questions: **Will people use this daily, or occasionally? Does it need to work without a connection? Does it need hardware a browser can't reach?** Three noes point firmly at a mobile-friendly website. A clear yes on offline or hardware points at a native app. Most businesses that *think* they need an app need a fast website first — and the ones that genuinely need an app usually need it for their field team, not their front page.
