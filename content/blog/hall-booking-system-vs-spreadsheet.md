Almost every organisation with rooms to book starts with a shared spreadsheet. And honestly? For a while, it works. One tab, a few colours, everyone can see it. If your venue booking never causes an argument, you may not need anything more — keep the spreadsheet.

But there's a predictable moment when the spreadsheet stops saving you time and starts costing it. Here's how to tell you've reached it, and what a real [hall booking system](/products/hall-booking/) does differently.

## The five ways a booking spreadsheet breaks

**1. Two people book the same room.** The spreadsheet has no idea a slot is taken until a human notices — and humans notice after both groups have shown up at the same door. A spreadsheet records bookings; it can't *prevent* conflicts. That single gap is why most organisations eventually move.

**2. Nobody knows who approved what.** A booking appears. Who made it? Who signed off? Was the VIP room really meant for a Tuesday sales call? A spreadsheet cell holds a name if you're lucky, and no history at all if you're not.

**3. The setup details live in someone's head.** Projector, seating layout, catering for twelve — the room is booked, but the requirements are in a WhatsApp message the facilities team never saw. So the room is free but not *ready*, which is its own kind of double booking.

**4. Editing rights are all-or-nothing.** To let people book, you let them edit the sheet — which means anyone can also delete a row, overwrite a formula, or drag a cell and silently shift twenty bookings. The more people who can book, the more fragile the file gets.

**5. Reporting means a manual afternoon.** "Which spaces are we actually using? Which sit empty? What should we charge?" The spreadsheet has the raw data but won't answer the question without someone spending half a day building a pivot table nobody trusts.

## What a booking system changes

The core difference isn't more features — it's that conflicts become *impossible* instead of merely unlikely. Every space sits on one shared calendar, so a slot that's taken simply can't be booked again. Requests route to the right approver with a full audit trail. Setup requirements attach to the booking, not to memory. People can book without being able to break the underlying data. And utilisation reports run themselves, so the estates team can see which rooms earn their keep without an afternoon of spreadsheet archaeology.

One university campus we worked with ran ninety bookable spaces through a shared inbox and a spreadsheet. The switch didn't just cut the double bookings — it removed the two days a month someone spent assembling utilisation reports by hand. ## Should you switch yet?

Stay on the spreadsheet if: you have a handful of spaces, one person owns all bookings, and conflicts never happen. Move when: multiple people book, multiple venues are in play, approvals matter, or you've had even one embarrassing double booking. The tool should match the complexity — and the moment two departments can book the same hall, the spreadsheet has quietly become the bottleneck.
